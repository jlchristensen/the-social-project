# The Social Project — Verified Statistics & Sources

Last updated: April 10, 2026

This document tracks every statistic used on The Social Project website, its source, and its verification status. All stats on the live site should trace back to a citation here.

## Stats Used on the Homepage (StatsSection)

### "50% of U.S. adults experience measurable loneliness"
- **Source:** U.S. Surgeon General's Advisory on the Healing Effects of Social Connection and Community (May 2023)
- **Details:** Approximately 50% of U.S. adults reported experiencing measurable levels of loneliness even before the COVID-19 pandemic. Young adults (18-34) consistently register the highest loneliness scores of any age group.
- **Link:** https://www.hhs.gov/about/news/2023/05/03/new-surgeon-general-advisory-raises-alarm-about-devastating-impact-epidemic-loneliness-isolation-united-states.html
- **Verified:** Yes

### "7h average daily screen time per person"
- **Source:** Multiple surveys including eMarketer, DemandSage, and autonomous.ai compilations (2025)
- **Details:** U.S. adults average 7 hours and 3 minutes of screen time per day. This figure has been stable since ~2021.
- **Link:** https://demandsage.com/screen-time-statistics
- **Verified:** Yes

### "70% drop in time young adults spend with friends since 2003"
- **Source:** American Time Use Survey data, cited in the Surgeon General's Advisory (2023)
- **Details:** For people ages 15 to 24, time spent in person with friends fell from approximately 150 minutes per day in 2003 to 40 minutes per day in 2020 — a nearly 70% decline.
- **Link:** https://www.hhs.gov/sites/default/files/sg-social-connection-graphic-trends.pdf
- **Verified:** Yes

### "5x increase in men reporting zero close friends since 1990"
- **Source:** Survey Center on American Life (American Enterprise Institute), May 2021
- **Details:** The percentage of men without any close friends jumped from 3% in 1990 to 15% in 2021 — a fivefold increase. Men with at least six close friends dropped from 55% to 27% over the same period.
- **Link:** https://www.americansurveycenter.org/research/the-state-of-american-friendship-change-challenges-and-loss
- **Verified:** Yes

## Stats Used in Blog Posts

### "Lacking social connection carries health risks comparable to smoking 15 cigarettes a day"
- **Used in:** "The Loneliness Epidemic" blog post
- **Source:** Holt-Lunstad, J., Smith, T. B., & Layton, J. B. (2010). Social Relationships and Mortality Risk: A Meta-analytic Review. *PLOS Medicine.*
- **Details:** Meta-analysis of 148 studies (308,849 participants). Found that stronger social relationships = 50% increased likelihood of survival. The 15-cigarette comparison benchmarks this effect size against known smoking mortality data. Important: the comparison is about "lacking social connection" broadly, not just the feeling of loneliness.
- **Link:** https://ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC2910600/
- **Clarification by author:** http://julianneholtlunstad.com/15-cigarettes
- **Verified:** Yes (with framing correction)

### "The Harvard Study of Adult Development" findings
- **Used in:** "The Art of Small Talk" blog post
- **Source:** Harvard Study of Adult Development, led by Robert Waldinger, MD
- **Details:** The world's longest study of adult life (85+ years, 2,500+ participants). Key finding: the quality of relationships is the single strongest predictor of happiness and health. People most satisfied in relationships at age 50 were the healthiest at age 80 — a better predictor than cholesterol levels.
- **Link:** https://www.robertwaldinger.com/harvard-study
- **Book:** *The Good Life* by Robert Waldinger and Marc Schulz (2023)
- **Verified:** Yes

### "About half of U.S. adults report experiencing measurable loneliness"
- **Used in:** "The Loneliness Epidemic" blog post
- **Source:** Same as homepage stat — Surgeon General Advisory, 2023
- **Verified:** Yes

### "Time spent with friends: 150 min/day (2003) → 40 min/day (2020)"

- **Verified:** Yes

### "Men with 6+ close friends dropped from 55% to 27% since 1990"

## Previously Used Stats (Removed — Inaccurate)

These were on the original version of the site and have been replaced:

| Original Stat | Problem | Replaced With |
|---|---|---|
| "61% of young adults report serious loneliness" | 61% was from a Cigna survey about *all* adults, not young adults specifically | "50% of U.S. adults experience measurable loneliness" |
| "30% drop in time spent with friends since 2014" | Real data shows ~70% drop since 2003 (understated and wrong timeframe) | "70% drop since 2003" |
| "1 in 3 adults have no close friends" | Pew (2023) found 8% have zero close friends — 1 in 3 was a major overstatement | "5x increase in men with zero close friends since 1990" |
| "Harvard shows 30% higher life satisfaction" | No such specific stat exists; fabricated number | Real Harvard Study findings (relationships predict health at 80) |
| "Average American hasn't made a new close friend in 5 years" | Cannot be verified — no major survey reports this specific stat | Replaced with men's close friend decline data |

## How to Use This Document

When adding new stats to the site:
1. Find the primary source (peer-reviewed study, government report, or major research center)
2. Add it to this document with a link
3. Mark it as verified
4. Use careful framing — "lacking social connection" is different from "loneliness"

When someone questions a stat:
1. Check this document for the source
2. Follow the link to verify it's still accurate
3. Update if the data has been revised
