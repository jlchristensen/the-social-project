# Community Tab Research Report

## Topic

How to build an engaging "Community" tab for The Social Project's website — featuring a daily question, an "answer before you read" participation gate, and Reddit-style community discussion.

## Why This Matters

The Social Project exists to ignite human connection for young adults. A community tab that actually gets people talking — not just scrolling — is the product living its mission. But most online communities fail. 90% of users on a typical platform are lurkers who never post. The "answer before you read" mechanic Jack is proposing directly attacks this problem: it flips the default from passive consumption to active participation. If executed well, this could be the defining feature that separates The Social Project from every other community brand.

This report covers what works, what to steal, and what to avoid — drawn from real platforms and current research.

## Key Findings

### 1. Reddit: The Gold Standard for Community Discussion

**What makes Reddit work:**

Reddit is the most successful community discussion platform ever built. Here's what drives it:

| Mechanic | How It Works | Why It Matters for TSP |
|----------|-------------|----------------------|
| **Upvotes/Downvotes** | Community votes content up or down, controlling visibility | Best content rises organically — no algorithm deciding what you see |
| **Threading** | Replies nest under parent comments, creating conversation trees | Lets discussions branch naturally — people can follow the threads that interest them |
| **Subreddits** | Topic-specific communities with their own rules and culture | Focus creates belonging — a subreddit of 10K people feels more intimate than a platform of 10M |
| **Karma** | Accumulated score from upvotes across all posts and comments | Lightweight gamification — gives contributors a reason to participate |
| **Velocity-based ranking** | Content that gets engagement *fast* gets boosted | Creates a "first mover advantage" that rewards early participation each day |

**Reddit's engagement formula:** The algorithm weights *engagement velocity* heavily — a post getting 50 upvotes in 1 hour outranks one getting 200 over 24 hours. This is why daily drops work: fresh content creates urgency.

**What Reddit gets wrong (and where TSP can be better):**

- **Toxic moderation:** Unpaid moderators often abuse power, muting users without explanation or banning people for participating in "wrong" communities. A curated community with clear, values-based guidelines avoids this.
- **Echo chambers:** Research from the University of Michigan found that moderators exhibit political bias in content removal, creating spaces where dissenting views are silenced. A daily question format naturally prevents this because everyone's answering the *same* prompt.
- **The lurker problem:** Reddit's 90-9-1 rule means 90% of users never contribute. The downvote system creates a "chilling effect" where people avoid sharing unpopular opinions. An answer-first gate solves this by making participation the default.
- **Intimidation factor:** Walking into a subreddit of millions and posting feels scary. A daily question lowers the bar — you're responding to a prompt, not broadcasting to the void.
- **No identity or warmth:** Reddit is pseudonymous and often hostile. TSP can be warm, personal, and identity-affirming.

**What to steal from Reddit:** Upvoting (but not downvoting), threaded replies, velocity-based ranking for surfacing the best answers, and the concept of focused community spaces.

### 2. "Answer Before You Read" — The Participation Gate

This is the most innovative part of Jack's concept. Here's what exists and what we can learn:

**Platforms using similar mechanics:**

| Platform | How They Gate | What Happens |
|----------|--------------|-------------|
| **Fishbowl** (by Glassdoor) | Verify with work email before posting; choose anonymous or identified for each post | Creates trusted, verified community. Users can post as "Anonymous at Google" or under their real name. 30K+ company-specific communities. |
| **BeReal** | Must post your own photo before you can see friends' photos | 68% of users post within 3 minutes of the daily notification. The gate *is* the product. |
| **NGL** | Share a prompt link; others must answer it to participate | Customizable question prompts drive engagement through Instagram Stories |
| **Fizz** (campus app) | Must verify with .edu email; can post anonymously after | Operates on 240+ campuses. Verified identity + anonymous posting = accountability without fear |
| **Quora** | Prominent "Write Your Answer" prompt appears before answers; platform actively invites users to answer questions in their areas | Shifted Quora from Q&A to a contribution-first platform |

**The key insight from BeReal:** BeReal proved that gating consumption behind contribution *works* — 68% of users contribute within 3 minutes of being prompted. But BeReal also showed the risk: when they added features that let people bypass the gate (posting outside the notification window), engagement dropped and they lost half their downloads in a year. **The gate must be sacred. Don't add workarounds.**

**How gating affects engagement quality:**

The 90-9-1 rule says that in most online communities, 90% of people just lurk, 9% contribute occasionally, and only 1% create content. Recent data from Higher Logic shows that smaller, well-designed communities can push active participation to 33% of members. An answer-first gate could push this even higher because:

1. **It eliminates the blank-page problem** — users aren't deciding *whether* to participate, just *how* to answer a specific question
2. **It creates psychological investment** — once someone writes their answer, they're more likely to read and engage with others' (the "IKEA effect" — we value things we helped create)
3. **It removes the intimidation of going first** — everyone's answer exists independently before they see the crowd
4. **It prevents groupthink** — because you answer before seeing others, your response is genuinely yours, not influenced by the top-voted answer

**Risks of the gate:**

- If the questions aren't good, people won't bother answering → churn
- If the gate feels like homework, it'll feel punishing → people leave
- If users can't skip a day without losing access, it creates resentment → need to handle "missed days" gracefully

### 3. Daily Question / Daily Prompt Mechanics

The daily cadence is a proven engagement driver. Here's who does it well and why:

**Wordle — The master class in daily habits:**

Wordle grew to 73+ million users using the simplest possible mechanic: one puzzle per day, same for everyone. The psychology behind it:

| Principle | How Wordle Uses It | How TSP Should Use It |
|-----------|-------------------|----------------------|
| **Scarcity** | Only one puzzle per day — you can't binge | One question per day. No archive binging. Today's question is *today's* conversation. |
| **Synchronized experience** | Everyone plays the same word | Everyone answers the same question — creates shared experience and common ground |
| **Social shareability** | The colored-square grid became a visual language | Answers could be shareable as quote cards on Instagram/TikTok |
| **Low commitment** | Takes 2-5 minutes | Answering one question should take 30-60 seconds |
| **Dopamine through completion** | Solving gives a hit of satisfaction | Submitting your answer and then seeing others' creates a two-phase reward |
| **No punishment for missing** | Miss a day? You just miss that word. No streak lost. | Don't punish missed days. Let people jump back in anytime. |

**We're Not Really Strangers (WNRS) — The connection brand:**

WNRS is the closest comparable brand to The Social Project. Their card game uses three escalating levels of questions — from surface-level to deeply personal. Their digital strategy:

- Built a text message community that grew from 14K to **1.2 million subscribers** in 10 months
- Sent 75 million texts, received 7 million direct responses
- Cross-promoted between text, TikTok, and Instagram
- Won the Shorty Award for Online Community

**What to steal from WNRS:** The escalation model (start with easy questions, earn access to deeper ones over time). The cross-platform loop (question on the site → share your answer on socials → drive traffic back). The warmth and vulnerability of the prompts.

**Hinge — Prompts that spark conversation:**

Hinge proved that structured prompts drive better conversations than open-ended ones. Key findings:

- Messages referencing specific prompt answers get **30% more responses** than generic messages
- They offer 100+ prompts across 10 categories, so users feel choice
- Top-performing prompts are personal but not too heavy: "The way to win me over is..." or "My simple pleasures..."

**What to steal from Hinge:** Prompt design. Questions should be personal enough to be interesting but light enough that answering doesn't feel like therapy. Balance between vulnerability and playfulness.

**BeReal — Daily notification as a ritual:**

BeReal's daily random notification created a Pavlovian response — 68% of users responded within 3 minutes. But the app also showed what happens when daily mechanics don't evolve: users got bored and downloads dropped 50%.

**What to steal from BeReal:** The daily notification trigger. What to avoid: letting the experience become repetitive. Vary question types, occasionally surprise users with themed days or collaborative prompts.

### 5. UX & Design Patterns

**Anonymous vs. Identified Posting:**

This is one of the most important design decisions for the community tab. Research shows three models:

| Model | Example | Pros | Cons |
|-------|---------|------|------|
| **Real names** | Facebook | Accountability, trust | People self-censor, vulnerable groups feel unsafe |
| **Stable pseudonyms** | Reddit, Fishbowl | Civility + freedom. Research shows this is *more civil than real names* | Less personal connection |
| **Full anonymity** | Yik Yak (original) | Maximum openness | Trolling, harassment, "troll's paradise" |

**Recommendation for TSP:** Use **stable pseudonyms with optional real names.** A Huffington Post study found that stable pseudonyms (verified account, consistent username) actually produced *more civil* behavior than real names. Users build a reputation tied to their handle without exposing their identity. Allow people to optionally show their first name or a nickname. Never require last names.

Fishbowl's model is worth copying: verified email to create an account (prevents throwaway troll accounts), then choose your display mode per post.

**Designing the "Gate" Without It Feeling Punishing:**

The answer-before-you-read mechanic needs to feel like an invitation, not a toll booth. Here's how:

1. **Frame it as a ritual, not a requirement.** "Today's question is waiting for you" > "You must answer before you can proceed"
2. **Keep it fast.** The question should take 30-60 seconds max to answer. If answering feels like writing an essay, people will bounce.
3. **Show a teaser.** "47 people have already shared their answer today" creates curiosity and social proof without revealing content.
4. **Celebrate the submission.** After they answer, show a satisfying animation and a message like "Your voice has been added. Here's what everyone else thinks..."
5. **Don't punish missed days.** If someone skips Tuesday, they should be able to answer Wednesday's question immediately. No "catch up on yesterday first" barrier.
6. **Allow short answers.** Even a sentence is participation. Don't enforce a minimum word count (but maybe a minimum character count of ~20 to prevent spam).

**Notification & Re-engagement Patterns:**

- **One notification per day, max.** Sending 1 push per week causes 10% of users to disable notifications. Less is more.
- **Personalize notifications.** Personalized push notifications are 4x more likely to be opened. "Sarah, today's question is about friendship — your answer from last week got 23 likes" beats "New daily question available."
- **Time it consistently.** Pick a time (e.g., 9am) and be reliable. Wordle worked because it reset at the same time every day. Predictability builds habits.
- **Use the two-phase reward loop.** Notification → answer question (reward #1: you contributed) → see others' answers (reward #2: social connection). This double-dopamine hit is more powerful than a single notification.

**Community Feed Design:**

- **Upvote only (no downvotes).** Reddit's downvote creates a chilling effect. For a community about vulnerability and connection, only allow positive signals (likes/upvotes, emoji reactions).
- **Sort by "most liked" as default, with options for "newest" and "most discussed."** Fizz's three-tab model (New, Top, Trending) is a clean pattern.
- **Show the question prominently at the top of the feed.** The question is the anchor — it should always be visible as context.
- **Replies/threading:** Allow one level of threading (reply to an answer), not infinite nesting. Deep nesting works on Reddit because people are there for hours; TSP sessions should be 2-5 minutes.
- **Emoji reactions on answers.** Quick, low-friction engagement. Locket's approach of emoji reactions that "rain down" as animations is delightful.

**Gamification That Fits a Connection Brand:**

- **Streaks** (days answered in a row) — but don't punish breaks. Show streaks as a personal stat, not a public leaderboard.
- **Badges for milestones** — "First Answer," "7-Day Streak," "100 Answers," "Most Liked Answer" — but keep them subtle. This isn't Duolingo.
- **"Connection Score"** — a private stat that tracks participation. Frame it around contribution ("You've shared your voice 47 times") rather than competition.
- Gamification boosts engagement by 100-150% and increases lifetime value by up to 7x, but only when it feels organic, not forced.

## Recommended UX Patterns for The Social Project's Community Tab

### The Core Loop

```
1. User opens Community tab (or gets daily notification)
2. Sees today's question — big, bold, centered
3. Writes their answer (30-60 seconds, free text)
4. Submits → celebration animation → "Here's what others said..."
5. Community feed unlocks — browse, upvote, reply
6. Optional: share their answer as a quote card on socials
```

### Information Architecture

```
Community Tab
├── Today's Question (hero, always at top)
│   ├── Question text
│   ├── Answer input field (if not yet answered)
│   └── Stats teaser: "73 people answered today"
├── Community Feed (unlocked after answering)
│   ├── Tabs: Top | New | Most Discussed
│   ├── Answer cards (avatar/handle, answer text, upvotes, reply count)
│   └── One-level reply threads
├── Your Profile (sidebar or tab)
│   ├── Your answers history
│   ├── Connection score / streak
│   └── Badges earned
└── Past Questions (archive, read-only)
    └── Browse by date or category
```

### Question Categories (Vary Daily)

| Category | Example | Vibe |
|----------|---------|------|
| **Icebreaker** | "What's the last thing that made you laugh out loud?" | Light, easy, high participation |
| **Reflection** | "What's something you've changed your mind about this year?" | Thoughtful, builds depth |
| **Connection** | "Describe a stranger who made your day better." | On-brand, mission-aligned |
| **Advice** | "What would you tell your 18-year-old self?" | Wisdom-sharing, community value |
| **Hot Take** | "Unpopular opinion: the best way to make friends as an adult is..." | Sparks debate, drives replies |
| **Gratitude** | "Name one person who showed up for you this week." | Warmth, positivity |
| **Hypothetical** | "If you could have dinner with any person, living or dead, who and why?" | Fun, reveals personality |

### Visual Design Principles

- **Warm, editorial feel** — not sterile social media. Think newsletter meets conversation.
- **The question should feel like a letter, not a form.** Big, beautiful typography. Maybe handwritten-style font for the question itself.
- **Answer cards should feel personal** — subtle avatar, first name or handle, maybe a soft background color that varies by user.
- **Generous whitespace.** This is a place to breathe and reflect, not a busy feed to scroll.
- **Mobile-first.** Most users will be on phones (especially from social media ads).

## Risks and Things to Watch Out For

### High Risk

| Risk | Why It Matters | Mitigation |
|------|---------------|------------|
| **Bad questions kill the feature** | If questions are boring, repetitive, or too heavy, people stop coming back | Build a question bank of 200+ questions across 7+ categories. A/B test early. Let the community suggest questions. |
| **Gate feels punishing** | If answering feels like homework, users bounce | Keep it fast (30-60 sec), frame as invitation not requirement, celebrate submissions |
| **Empty room problem** | Early on, answering and seeing 3 other responses feels dead | Seed with 10-20 founding members. Show questions even when feed is small. Consider showing total answers without revealing content until gate is passed. |
| **Content moderation** | Anonymity + vulnerability = potential for harassment | Automate basic moderation (profanity filter), have a report button, establish clear community guidelines, consider human review for flagged content |

### Medium Risk

| Risk | Why It Matters | Mitigation |
|------|---------------|------------|
| **Single-mechanic fatigue** | BeReal lost half its users when the daily mechanic got stale | Vary question types, add occasional themed days, introduce collaborative prompts, evolve the format quarterly |
| **Notification burnout** | More than 1 notification/week causes 10% to disable | One daily notification, make it warm and personal, let users choose their notification time |
| **Scope creep** | Adding DMs, groups, events, etc. too early fragments the experience | Launch with ONLY the daily question + feed. Add features based on what users ask for. |
| **Privacy concerns** | Young adults are increasingly privacy-conscious | Be transparent about data use, don't require real names, let users delete their answers |

### Low Risk (But Worth Noting)

| Risk | Why It Matters | Mitigation |
|------|---------------|------------|
| **Technical complexity** | Real-time feeds, authentication, moderation require backend work | Start simple: Supabase for database + auth, basic CRUD for answers, no real-time initially |
| **Platform vs. feature** | Is this a tab on the site, or does it deserve its own app? | Start as a tab. If it takes off, consider a standalone experience. |

## Next Steps

### Immediate (This Week)

- [ ] **Build a question bank.** Write 50 questions across the 7 categories above. This is Jack's superpower — he knows the audience. Aim for questions that are easy to answer but interesting to read others' answers to.
- [ ] **Design the wireframe.** Sketch the core loop: question → answer input → submission → feed. Keep it to one screen. Use the information architecture above as a starting frame.
- [ ] **Decide on identity model.** Recommendation: email-verified accounts with display names (not real names required). This is the Fishbowl/Fizz model that balances accountability with safety.

### Soon (Next 2 Weeks)

- [ ] **Build the MVP.** The minimum feature set is: daily question display, answer submission, answer-before-read gate, community feed with upvotes. Use Supabase (already in the project) for database and auth.
- [ ] **Seed the community.** Recruit 10-20 founding members from the email list or personal network. They answer first each day to ensure the feed never feels empty.
- [ ] **Set up basic moderation.** Profanity filter + report button + community guidelines page.

### Later (Month 2+)

- [ ] **Add notifications.** Daily email or push notification with the question. Personalize based on past engagement.
- [ ] **Add social sharing.** Let users share their answer (or a favorite answer) as a branded quote card to Instagram Stories.
- [ ] **Add gamification.** Streaks, badges, connection score. Keep it light and aligned with the brand.
- [ ] **Add question suggestions.** Let the community propose questions. Vote on which ones become the daily question.
- [ ] **Analyze engagement.** Track: daily answer rate, return rate, average answers per question, most-upvoted answer patterns, time spent in feed. Use this data to improve question quality.
