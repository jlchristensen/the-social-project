# Content Integrity Standard

## Why This Matters

The Social Project is a brand built on authenticity. If we tell people to "be real" but have fabricated statistics on our homepage, we lose all credibility. One skeptical reader with Google can undo months of trust-building. Every fact we publish is a promise to our audience that we've done our homework.

This standard is non-negotiable. It applies to every agent and every person who creates content for The Social Project.

## The Rule

**Every statistic, study reference, and factual claim displayed on the site must be:**

1. **Real** — sourced from an actual, verifiable study, survey, or report
2. **Accurate** — correctly stated (not exaggerated, rounded misleadingly, or taken out of context)
3. **Cited** — traceable to a primary source (peer-reviewed research, government reports, major research institutions)
4. **Current** — based on the most recent available data (check that findings haven't been updated or revised)
5. **Documented** — recorded in `docs/research/verified-statistics.md` with a link to the source

## What Counts as a Valid Source

### Tier 1 (Preferred)
- Peer-reviewed journal articles (PLOS Medicine, JAMA, etc.)
- Government reports and advisories (Surgeon General, CDC, Bureau of Labor Statistics)
- Major research institutions (Pew Research, Gallup, Harvard, Stanford)
- Official datasets (American Time Use Survey, Census Bureau)

### Tier 2 (Acceptable with context)
- Reports from credible research centers (Survey Center on American Life, McKinsey, etc.)
- Well-sourced journalism from major outlets (NYT, WSJ, The Atlantic)
- Industry reports from established firms (eMarketer, Statista — note methodology)

### Tier 3 (Use with caution)
- Blog posts or opinion pieces — cite the underlying study, not the blog
- Social media posts — never use as a primary source
- AI-generated content — always verify independently
- Wikipedia — follow the citations to the primary source

### Not Acceptable
- Unsourced claims ("studies show..." with no specific study)
- Paraphrased statistics from memory
- Numbers that "sound right" but can't be traced to a source
- Competitor websites (they may have the same problem we had)

## How to Apply This

### When Writing New Content

1. **Before adding any stat:** Find the primary source and verify the exact number
2. **When citing a study:** Use the actual finding, not a telephone-game version of it
3. **When framing data:** Be precise about what was measured ("lacking social connection" is different from "loneliness")
4. **After writing:** Add every stat to `docs/research/verified-statistics.md` with its source link
5. **When in doubt:** Use softer language ("research suggests...") rather than fabricating a specific number

### When Reviewing Existing Content

If you encounter a stat on the site that isn't in `docs/research/verified-statistics.md`:
1. Stop and verify it before moving on
2. If it checks out, add it to the document
3. If it doesn't check out, flag it and replace it

### For AI-Generated Content

AI models (including the ones powering these agents) sometimes generate plausible-sounding but fabricated statistics. This is a known behavior. Every agent must:
- **Never invent statistics**, even if they "sound about right"
- **Always verify** any factual claim before it goes on the site
- **Use web search** to find real sources when stats are needed
- **Say "I don't have a verified source for this"** rather than guessing

## Examples

### Good
> "About half of U.S. adults report experiencing measurable loneliness, with young adults consistently scoring the highest of any age group (U.S. Surgeon General Advisory, 2023)."

### Bad
> "Studies show that 73% of millennials feel disconnected from their peers."

*Why it's bad: No specific study cited. "73%" sounds precise but is invented. "Studies show" is a red flag that usually means "I made this up."*

### Good
> "A landmark meta-analysis of 148 studies found that lacking social connection carries health risks comparable to smoking 15 cigarettes a day (Holt-Lunstad et al., 2010, PLOS Medicine)."

### Bad
> "Loneliness is worse than smoking two packs a day."

*Why it's bad: The original research says "up to 15 cigarettes" (not two packs), it's about "lacking social connection" (not just loneliness), and there's no citation.*

## The Verified Statistics Document

All stats used on the site are tracked in:

**`docs/research/verified-statistics.md`**

This document includes:
- The exact stat as it appears on the site
- The primary source with a link
- Verification status
- A record of stats that were removed and why

Any agent adding content to the site must update this document when new stats are introduced.

## Accountability

This isn't about being overly academic or slowing things down. It's about respecting the audience enough to tell them the truth. The Social Project asks people to be authentic — the least we can do is hold ourselves to the same standard.

When a stat needs to be compelling, the real numbers are almost always more powerful than made-up ones. The truth is dramatic enough.
